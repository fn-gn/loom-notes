\section*{The one idea, and how to read these notes}
\addcontentsline{toc}{section}{The one idea · how to read}
\warmth{0}

\begin{strand}
A signal is a sum of \keyword{pure tones} $e^{2\pi i\xi x}$, and the Fourier transform
$\widehat f(\xi)$ reads off how much of each is present. The single idea of these notes:
\keyword{the Fourier basis is the eigenbasis of shift}. Because every translation-invariant
operation is diagonal in that basis, the hardest things in one domain (convolution,
differentiation, solving a PDE) become the easiest things in the other (just multiplication).
\end{strand}

\block{The master dictionary: in space $\leftrightarrow$ in frequency (fill it in)}
\noindent\begin{tabularx}{\linewidth}{@{}L L@{}}
\toprule
{\color{indigo}operation on $f(x)$} & {\color{madder}what it does to $\widehat f(\xi)$}\\
\midrule
shift $f(x-a)$                & multiply by a phase $e^{\fillin[1.6cm]}$ \;(§\ref{sec:dict})\\
convolution $(f*g)(x)$        & ordinary product $\widehat f(\xi)\,\fillin[1.2cm]$ \;(the headline)\\
derivative $f'(x)$            & multiply by $\fillin[1.6cm]$\\
scaling $f(ax)$              & stretch/shrink $\tfrac{1}{\abs a}\widehat f(\xi/a)$\\
energy $\int\abs{f}^2$        & equal energy $\int\abs{\widehat f}^2$ \;(Parseval, §\ref{sec:why})\\
narrow in $x$               & $\fillin[2.5cm]$ wide in $\xi$ \;(uncertainty, §\ref{sec:why})\\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
